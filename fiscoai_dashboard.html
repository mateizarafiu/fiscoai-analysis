Option-by-Option Legal Analysis
A structured assessment of financial mechanics, legal obligations, and stakeholder risk-return profiles for each option.

Option 1 — Series B Extension

Existing Series B investor · €10M in two tranches · Participating preferred · €30M pre-money

Full ratchet anti-dilution explained

The full ratchet is the most aggressive anti-dilution mechanism available. If FiscoAI raises a future round below €461.54/share — its current Series B price — the Series B Extension investor's conversion price is reset to the lower price, regardless of volume. The company must issue additional shares to compensate the investor at zero cost, diluting all other shareholders entirely at their expense.

Example: A Series C at €300/share triggers a retroactive reset from €461.54 to €300. The Series B Extension investor receives free additional shares worth millions — paid for by founders and early investors who bear the full dilution burden.

Real-World Case Studies
Each option maps to a documented transaction that illustrates its structural risk profile in practice.
Option 1 parallel
Groupon — Series D extension (2010)
DST Global & Andreessen Horowitz · Pre-IPO equity extension

Groupon accepted a milestone-structured equity extension from its lead Series D investors at a flat valuation, with participating preferred shares and strong governance rights. Like Option 1, the extension was priced during a period of commercial underperformance, and existing investors used the distress to negotiate disproportionate protections.

At the 2011 IPO at $20/share, the layered liquidation preferences absorbed the majority of proceeds for preferred holders. Co-founder Andrew Mason's common stock received a fraction of the headline valuation — a direct consequence of the participatory preferred structure, precisely analogous to FiscoAI's Option 1.

Lesson: participating preferred at distressed valuation permanently mortgages founder and early investor upside.
Option 2 parallel
Klarna — Pre-Series D convertible bridge (2021–22)
Existing backers · PIK convertible · Pre-IPO structure

Facing a compressed IPO timeline and market uncertainty, Klarna raised convertible instruments from existing investors with PIK interest and a conversion discount. Governance remained fully undisturbed during the bridge period — exactly as in Option 2.

When the IPO was delayed and Klarna raised a down-round at $6.7B (vs. $45.6B peak), the convertible notes converted at deeply discounted prices. This crystallised the maturity cliff risk — but crucially, the company survived and filed for IPO at ~$15B in 2024.

Lesson: Option 2 is the right instrument if the company reaches a qualifying event. Without one, the cliff is real and costly.
Option 3 parallel
Peloton — Debt facility restructuring (2022–23)
JP Morgan & Goldman Sachs · $750M high-yield facility

Peloton — executing a pivot from D2C to B2B enterprise while managing a cash crisis — secured a large debt facility with cash maintenance covenants and IP-adjacent collateral. Annual interest payments competed directly with turnaround investment, mirroring Option 3's structural tension for FiscoAI.

Covenant breaches triggered a 2023 restructuring requiring significant equity dilution and governance concessions. Lenders' collateral position gave them decisive leverage in the restructuring — precisely the dynamic created by Option 3's IP pledge.

Lesson: cash-pay debt on an unprofitable pivot company compounds distress. IP collateral is an existential threat on any default event.

Recommendation & Proposed Amendments
Our verdict as legal and financial counsel to FiscoAI, including specific negotiating amendments to the recommended instrument.

✓ Recommendation: Accept Option 2 — Convertible Debt (StarFund)

Option 2 is the only structure that simultaneously addresses FiscoAI's immediate capital needs (€10M fully committed, no milestone gates), preserves governance autonomy through the turnaround period (no veto rights, no new board seat), and maintains a viable pathway to a Series C on competitive terms (no reserved matter over capital raises, no full ratchet anti-dilution).

FiscoAI's core problem is not a lack of long-term potential — it is a lack of time. Option 2 solves the time problem with the least structural damage to the cap table and governance framework.

Why not Option 1
  • Flat €30M pre-money valuation signals no progress — anchors Series C at a weak baseline
  • Full ratchet anti-dilution requires a formal waiver before any Series C investor will engage
  • 2× participating preference captures 90% of exit proceeds at current valuation — founders receive €0.68M on 10.25% stake at a €45M exit
  • Five-category veto right including capital increases blocks every future financing round without Series B consent
  • Founder forfeiture clause creates legal exposure and talent retention risk
Why not Option 3
  • IP collateralisation is an existential risk — any covenant breach gives VNTR the right to seize the core technology asset
  • 12% cash interest (€960K–€1.44M/yr) directly competes with the R&D and commercial expansion investment the pivot requires
  • EBITDA breakeven within 24 months for Tranche 2 is unrealistic for a company currently unprofitable and pivoting to enterprise sales
  • IP lien must be released before any Series C can close — adds cost, time, and negotiation complexity
  • Total cost of borrowing: ~€18.24M on a €12M facility over 5 years
Recommended amendments to Option 2

Accept Option 2 subject to two targeted amendments that counsel should seek to negotiate with StarFund:

Amendment 1 — Reduce maturity conversion preference from 1.5× to 1× non-participating

The 1.5× participating preference on maturity conversion is disproportionate to StarFund's risk profile at that stage. A 1× non-participating preference is market-standard for bridge notes converting at maturity and would materially improve the distributional position of founders and early investors in an exit following maturity conversion. Proposed counter: offer StarFund a slightly higher conversion discount (25% instead of 20% at maturity) in exchange for this reduction.

Amendment 2 — Cap the "most senior class" rights escalation

The current clause allows StarFund's converted shares to automatically accrete to the full rights of whatever most senior class exists at conversion — including rights negotiated exclusively by future Series C investors. This creates a free-rider problem and adds complexity to every future financing. Proposed counter: cap StarFund's accreted rights at 1× non-participating preference, or require a buyout option of the note at a modest premium as part of any Series C closing.

These two amendments preserve StarFund's legitimate return for early-stage risk while removing the two structural features most likely to complicate a Series C negotiation. StarFund, as a SaaS-focused fund, has strong commercial incentives to facilitate a successful qualifying event — these amendments align its formal rights with its economic interests.
Final verdict

FiscoAI's problem is not a lack of long-term potential — it is a lack of time. Option 2 solves this with the least structural damage: full capital committed unconditionally, governance autonomy preserved, cap table clean for a Series C. The maturity cliff is real, but it is within management's control to address through execution of the enterprise pivot. The governance and distributional harms of Option 1 are not within management's control once signed. Option 2 is our recommendation.
