Option-by-Option Analysis
Financial mechanics, legal obligations, and stakeholder profiles. All numbers from the Excel model.

Option 1 — Series B Extension

Existing investor · €10M in two tranches · 2× participating preferred · Full ratchet anti-dilution

Full ratchet anti-dilution

The most aggressive anti-dilution available. If FiscoAI raises a future round below €461.54/share, the Series B Extension price resets to that lower price — regardless of volume. The company must issue free shares to the investor, diluting everyone else. This provision appears in fewer than 15% of comparable European VC transactions.

Example: Series C at €300/share → Series B Extension investor's price retroactively resets from €461.54 to €300. Free shares issued at the expense of founders and early investors. A weighted-average ratchet (market standard in 85%+ of EU deals) would have distributed this burden far more equitably.

Real-World Case Studies
Each option maps to a documented transaction that illustrates its structural risk profile in practice.
Option 1 parallel
Groupon — Series D (2010)
DST Global & Andreessen Horowitz · Pre-IPO equity extension

Groupon accepted a milestone-structured equity extension at a flat valuation with participating preferred shares and strong governance rights. Like Option 1, the extension was priced during commercial underperformance, and existing investors used the distress to negotiate disproportionate protections. At the 2011 IPO at $20/share, layered liquidation preferences absorbed the majority of proceeds for preferred holders — directly analogous to FiscoAI's Option 1 waterfall.

Lesson: participating preferred at distressed valuation permanently mortgages founder and early investor upside. The Groupon structure maps exactly to Option 1's 2× preference + participation combination.
Option 2 parallel
Klarna — Convertible bridge (2021–22)
Existing backers · PIK · Pre-IPO

Klarna raised convertible instruments ahead of a planned IPO with PIK interest and a 20% conversion discount. Governance remained undisturbed at signing — identical to Option 2's structure. When the IPO was delayed and Klarna raised a down-round at $6.7B (vs. $45.6B peak), the notes converted at discounted prices, crystallising the maturity cliff risk. The company subsequently filed for IPO at ~$15B in 2024.

Lesson: Option 2 works when the company achieves a qualifying event. Without one, the maturity cliff is real and costly — as Klarna's down-round conversion demonstrated.
Option 3 parallel
Peloton — Debt facility (2022–23)
JP Morgan & Goldman · $750M high-yield

Peloton, pivoting from D2C to B2B enterprise while managing a cash crisis, secured a $750M debt facility with cash maintenance covenants and IP-adjacent collateral — mirroring VNTR's covenant structure precisely. Annual interest payments competed directly with turnaround investment. Covenant breaches triggered a 2023 restructuring requiring significant equity dilution and governance concessions. Lenders' collateral position gave them decisive leverage.

Lesson: cash-pay debt on an unprofitable pivot compounds distress. IP collateral becomes a weapon in restructuring negotiations.

Recommendation & Verdict
Final verdict as legal and financial counsel to FiscoAI, including scoring matrix and proposed amendments.

✓ Recommendation: Accept Option 2 — Convertible Debt (StarFund)

Option 2 is the only structure that simultaneously addresses FiscoAI's immediate capital needs (€10M fully committed, no milestones), preserves governance autonomy through the turnaround (no veto rights, no board seat), and maintains a viable Series C pathway (no reserved matter over capital raises, no full ratchet).

FiscoAI's problem is not a lack of long-term potential — it is a lack of time. Option 2 solves this with the least structural damage to the cap table and governance framework.

Two amendments to negotiate on Option 2

Amendment 1 — Reduce maturity preference from 1.5× to 1× non-participating

The 1.5× participating preference at maturity conversion is disproportionate to StarFund's risk profile. A 1× non-participating preference is market standard for bridge notes converting at maturity. Proposed counter-offer: increase the maturity conversion discount from 20% to 25% in exchange for this reduction.

Amendment 2 — Cap the "most senior class" rights escalation

The current clause lets StarFund's converted shares automatically inherit whatever terms the Series C investor negotiates — a free-rider problem. Proposed counter-offer: cap StarFund's accreted rights at 1× non-participating, or include a buyout option at a modest premium as part of any Series C closing.

These two amendments preserve StarFund's legitimate return for early-stage risk while removing the structural features most likely to complicate a Series C negotiation. StarFund, as a SaaS-focused UK fund, has strong commercial incentives to facilitate a qualifying event — these amendments align its formal rights with its economic interests.
Final verdict — Team 21

Option 2 solves FiscoAI's time problem with the least structural damage: full capital committed unconditionally, governance autonomy preserved, cap table clean for a Series C. The maturity cliff is real — but it is within management's control through execution of the enterprise pivot. The governance and distributional harms of Option 1 are not within management's control once signed. The IP existential risk of Option 3 is not acceptable for a currently unprofitable company in turnaround mode. Option 2 is our recommendation, subject to the two amendments above.
